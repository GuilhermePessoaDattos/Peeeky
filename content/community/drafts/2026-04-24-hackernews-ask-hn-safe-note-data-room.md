## Thread Summary

A hardware startup founder raising $2M (seed stage, pre-revenue) is puzzled that some investors are requesting formal data rooms and investor memorandums. Most investors in their round only asked for a deck, executive summary, and whitepaper. They question whether data room requests signal unsophisticated investors, citing YC's guidance that excessive due diligence requests at seed are a red flag. One commenter agreed that a shared folder is sufficient and that investors insisting on more may not be good partners.

## Proposed Response

The YC framing is right for most cases, but worth adding some nuance: the question is less "does the investor want a data room" and more "what are they actually asking for."

A lot of seed-stage investors use "data room" loosely to mean "put everything in one organized place so I don't have to keep emailing you for cap table, financials, and corporate docs." That's completely reasonable and takes 2 hours to set up in Google Drive or Notion. That's different from an investor who wants watermarked documents, NDA gating, and granular access logs — that behavior signals someone who's either late-stage-trained or doing more diligence than the deal size warrants.

For a $2M seed hardware round, a reasonable data room has:
- Current pitch deck
- Cap table (a Carta export or even a clean spreadsheet)
- 12–18 months of financials (or projections if pre-revenue)
- Incorporation docs + any existing SAFEs
- Key contracts or LOIs if you have them
- Brief competitive landscape

That's it. If an investor keeps asking for more after that, they're either genuinely diligencing deeply (positive) or they're stalling (a separate problem no data room will solve).

The bigger question is whether investors can see *what they're engaging with* after you send the room link. Google Drive gives you no signal — you don't know if someone opened the financials or never looked at the deck. That engagement visibility is increasingly part of how founders prioritize follow-up, especially when you're managing 20+ conversations at once.
