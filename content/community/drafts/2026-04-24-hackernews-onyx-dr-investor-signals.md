## Thread Summary

Founders built Onyx Data Rooms targeting early- and growth-stage startups during fundraising. Their key insight: existing VDRs focus on later-stage processes, while early-stage founders need clarity about which investors are genuinely engaged vs. just clicking through. Features include unlimited data rooms, per-investor document analytics, and built-in Q&A that links investor questions back to relevant documents. The thread invites community feedback on how founders currently handle investor signaling.

## Proposed Response

The engagement-vs-clicking problem is real and one of the most useful signals in a raise. A few thoughts from experience watching founders use document analytics:

**Engagement patterns that actually matter:**
- Return visits matter more than first-view duration. An investor who comes back to your cap table or financials section three days later is deeper in diligence than one who spent four minutes on the deck once.
- Page-level drop-off is a better signal than total time. If you see that 80% of viewers exit at the Team slide, that's a structural deck problem. If one specific investor exits there, that's probably a fit issue with the founding team composition.
- Forwarding is the most underappreciated signal. When a deck gets sent from a principal's email domain to a partner's, the deal is moving.

**On the Q&A feature:** Linking investor questions to documents is smart. One thing to watch — founders sometimes panic when they see an investor ask "what's your burn rate?" if they already put that in the deck. You might build in a "this question was answered on page X" prompt so founders understand whether it's a comprehension gap or genuine digging.

**One product question:** Are you exposing the engagement analytics to the investor too, or only to the founder? There are tools that let both sides see activity, which changes the dynamic significantly.

The timing is good — DocSend removed their free tier in early 2025, which pushed a lot of early-stage founders off the platform and into re-evaluating what they actually need from a data room. That's a real cohort looking for something like this.
