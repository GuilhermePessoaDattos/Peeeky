## Proposed Response

This is a real pain — I've seen indie founders sending investor updates via a BCC'd Gmail list with a Google Doc attached, which is fine until you have 30+ investors and no idea who actually read anything.

A few things that came up as I thought about this workflow:

**What actually makes investor updates useful:**
The best updates I've seen are short (under 400 words), structured (numbers first, then narrative), and honest about what's not working. Investors who receive honest "here's what's hard right now" updates tend to be the most useful when you need a connection or a quick reference.

**The part that's still messy in most tools:**
Knowing whether investors actually engaged with the update. If you send an update and two people open it immediately and five don't open it for two weeks, that tells you something about who's paying attention. That's where document/link tracking layered into the CRM becomes genuinely useful — not just "sent" but "read by, on this date."

**On the pricing point:**
The $700-1K/year gap between free tools (Notion + email) and enterprise CRMs is exactly where indie SaaS lives. The open-source option is smart — it lowers the trust barrier for people sharing sensitive cap table and revenue data with a tool they don't fully know yet.

For the document side of this (sharing pitch decks or board materials with investors and knowing who read what), we've been building something called Peeeky (peeeky.com) that does per-recipient link tracking for documents. Not a CRM, but it handles the "did they actually look at it" layer. The two tools could complement each other well for early-stage founders managing their full investor comms stack.

Good luck with the launch — building in public in this space means you'll get very direct feedback from the exact people who need it most.
