## Thread Summary

A discussion questioning whether the pitch deck industrial complex — coaching, design services, polished templates — trains founders to optimize for investor perception (the 5-minute impression) rather than for building durable businesses (the 5-year reality). The thread draws out tension between decks as a "forcing function for clarity" vs. decks as a performance that selects for narrative skill over operational depth.

---

## Proposed Response

The deck-as-performance critique is valid, but I think the real damage happens in the distribution layer, not the creation layer.

Most founders obsess over slide 5 vs. slide 6 ordering, then send a PDF attachment and have no idea what happens next. The VC forwards it internally, their associate skims it, someone marks it as "read" in Salesforce, and the founder follows up blind. That feedback vacuum is where pitch culture gets really dysfunctional — founders can't distinguish "they hated it" from "they haven't looked yet" from "they're circling back next week," so they either over-follow-up or go silent.

The decks that actually work tend to share a few traits that have nothing to do with design:

1. **They answer the investor's pre-flight checklist before the meeting.** Team, market size, differentiation, traction, ask — in the first six slides, no exceptions.
2. **They leave room for conversation.** The best decks I've seen treat slides as chapter headings, not essays.
3. **Founders using tracked links (not PDF attachments) close the feedback loop faster.** When you can see that a partner spent 8 minutes on the financial model but 12 seconds on the market size slide, you know what to fix before the next send — or what to lead with in the follow-up call.

The pitch coaching industry probably does select for performance over substance. But the operational fix isn't to ditch the deck — it's to treat the deck as a live instrument you instrument and iterate, not a PDF you fire and forget.

*(Disclosure: I work on Peeeky, a document-tracking tool for exactly this use case — so take the above with the appropriate grain of salt, but the data-driven iteration point stands regardless of what tool you use.)*
