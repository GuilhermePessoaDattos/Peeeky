## Context

Posted April 13, 2026, linking to Steve Blank's article "Your Startup Is Probably Dead on Arrival" (steveblank.com/2026/03/17/). The article argues that the bottleneck has shifted: it's no longer engineering capacity but "judgment, customer insight, and distribution." He uses a drone company that missed a $20B defense VC market shift while "heads down" building.

The thread has 141 comments and 195 points. Dominant comment themes are skeptical: multiple commenters push back on AI-generated-feeling prose, argue the bottleneck was never just coding, and note that macroeconomic factors (high interest rates, recession) explain funding difficulty better than AI disruption does. karolist notes founders are still constrained by system design, UX, and pricing decisions. kristianc observes that startup failure rates haven't improved across decades of lean methodology.

The relevance to Peeeky is indirect but real: the "distribution" and "customer insight" bottleneck Blank identifies is partly a document and communication problem. Founders are bad at knowing whether investors and prospects are actually engaging with what they send — or whether they're being politely ghosted — until it's too late to course-correct.

## Proposed Response

The distribution point in Blank's article doesn't get enough specificity, and I think the pushback in this thread is partly a result of that vagueness.

When he says the bottleneck is now "distribution," one concrete version of that problem is: founders don't know what's happening after they send something. You send a deck to twelve investors, hear back from two, and the standard interpretation is "ten passed." But the reality is often more granular — some never opened it, two looked at slide 1 and closed it, one spent twelve minutes on the financials and then went quiet, one forwarded it internally and you have no idea.

The same thing happens with proposals and sales docs. The team spends a week building a proposal, sends it, and then follows up with "just checking in" emails that land badly because they have no signal on whether the prospect is engaged, overwhelmed, or just waiting for budget approval.

The assumption that was probably true two years ago: a great product in the right category would find investors and customers on the strength of the content alone. The assumption that's less true now: you can operate blind on distribution and make up for it with persistence.

Blank's advice to "test more hypotheses in parallel" only works if you have feedback loops short enough to actually update on. A lot of founders are running experiments with 3-4 week lag times because they can't see engagement signals on the docs they're sending — which makes the rapid iteration he's advocating basically impossible.
