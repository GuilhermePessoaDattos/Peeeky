## Context

Real HN thread posted April 3, 2026 by ali_chherawalla, presenting RightSuite — a GTM simulation platform that tests pricing, messaging, and audience fit against synthetic buyer populations before launch. The core pitch: compress months of real-world iteration into weeks by validating assumptions against simulated buyers first. Directly relevant because the problem RightSuite addresses (not knowing how buyers engage with your pitch before sending it) is adjacent to what Peeeky solves on the *post-send* side — knowing how they actually engage after you've sent it. Thread is fresh (posted today), active, and the discussion will likely center on simulation quality, feedback signal reliability, and when synthetic feedback diverges from real buyers.

## Proposed Response

The problem you're solving is real — early GTM mistakes are expensive not because of the direct cost but because of the opportunity cost. Three months spent on wrong positioning means three months of deals that didn't close.

One thing I'd push back on gently: the "70% of the way there" framing might be optimistic for pricing specifically. Simulated buyer willingness-to-pay tends to diverge significantly from real behavior because price anchoring effects only emerge when real money is on the table. The buyer persona who says "$500/month feels right" in a simulation often responds very differently when facing an actual purchase decision with budget approval involved.

Where I think synthetic feedback tends to be most reliable is *message clarity* — whether your pitch actually communicates what you think it communicates. That's a linguistics/comprehension problem more than a valuation problem, and it doesn't require real economic stakes to surface. Positioning tests ("does this resonate?") are more trustworthy than pricing tests ("would you pay X?").

The complementary problem worth thinking about: once you're past the GTM simulation phase and actually sending proposals, decks, or one-pagers to real buyers, the question flips — did they read it, which sections got attention, what were they unsure about? Simulation helps you go in with a better message. Engagement analytics on the actual sends tell you how the real message lands. The two are genuinely complementary, not redundant.

Curious what the retention / re-run rate looks like — do users typically run one simulation per product or iterate through multiple variants on the same question?
