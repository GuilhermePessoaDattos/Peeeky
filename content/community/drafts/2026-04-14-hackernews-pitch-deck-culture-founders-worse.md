## Context

Posted April 11, 2026 by an early-stage fund manager. The OP argues that while pitch decks have improved aesthetically year-over-year, the underlying businesses have not improved proportionally. The core observation: "founders optimize for the 5-minute impression. Investors evaluate for the 5-year business."

Key comments draw out several connected ideas: codingdave argues that treating fundraising as the primary goal — rather than the business itself — is the root problem. chinhqtran (the OP) offers a sharp diagnostic: founders who survived by selling before raising talk about specific named customers and real objections; "pitch mode" founders talk about abstract personas. simianwords invokes Goodhart's Law — once the deck became the optimization target, it stopped being an accurate map of the business. The thread has strong engagement and a genuine intellectual vibe, not just startup advice platitudes.

There is a direct opening here for Peeeky: one commenter (whycombinetor) specifically asks the OP whether their own pitch analyzer product reflects these principles. The OP acknowledged weaknesses the tool surfaced. The thread is actively examining the relationship between the deck-as-artifact and what it actually signals to investors — which is exactly where analytics help or hurt depending on how founders use them.

## Proposed Response

The Goodhart's Law point cuts deep here, and I think it extends into how founders use engagement analytics too.

Deck analytics (time-per-slide, which pages get re-read, where people drop off) are genuinely useful — but only if you treat them as diagnostic signals rather than optimization targets. If a founder sees investors spending 45 seconds on the traction slide and immediately revises it to be more visually striking, they're doing the same thing the OP is critiquing: gaming the impression rather than addressing the underlying question the investor is actually sitting with.

The more valuable use of that data is quieter: which slide caused the conversation to end? If three different investors each spent 8 seconds on your team slide before closing the link, that's probably not about the slide design. It's a signal that something upstream — maybe the problem framing, maybe the market size claim — left them unconvinced enough to even want to evaluate the team.

chinhqtran's customer test is the clearest version of this: the deck should be a compressed version of what you already know from real customer conversations, not a narrative you're constructing to fill the template. When it's the latter, no amount of slide polish or analytics will paper over the absence of insight.

The founders I've seen use engagement data well are the ones who already had strong customer conviction — they used the analytics to figure out which parts of their real story weren't landing in the deck format, not to discover what story to tell.
